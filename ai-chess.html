About Chess

Chess is a two-player strategy board game played on a checkered board with 64 squares arranged in an 8×8 grid. Each player begins with 16 pieces: one king, one queen, two rooks, two knights, two bishops, and eight pawns.

The game captures the essence of medieval warfare, where the goal is to checkmate your opponent's king by placing it under an inescapable threat of capture. Along with Go, chess is one of the world's most ancient and enduring games of pure strategy.

Chess requires and develops concentration, critical thinking, abstract reasoning, problem solving, pattern recognition, strategic planning, creativity, and evaluation – making it not just a game but a valuable mental exercise.

Learn Chess Basics

Chess Pieces and Movements

  • King: Moves one square in any direction. The most important piece – when it's in checkmate, you lose.
  • Queen: The most powerful piece, moving any number of squares diagonally, horizontally, or vertically.
  • Rook: Moves any number of squares horizontally or vertically. Great for controlling files and ranks.
  • Bishop: Moves any number of squares diagonally, always staying on the same color square.
  • Knight: Moves in an L-shape: two squares in one direction and then one square perpendicular. Can jump over pieces.
  • Pawn: Moves forward one square (or two on its first move), but captures diagonally. Can be promoted when reaching the opposite end.

Basic Rules

  1. White always moves first.
  2. Players take turns making one move at a time.
  3. A piece is captured when an opponent's piece moves to its square.
  4. The king can never be captured, only put in check.
  5. If your king is in check, you must get out of check on your next move.
  6. Checkmate occurs when a king is in check and cannot escape, ending the game.
  7. Stalemate occurs when a player has no legal moves but their king is not in check, resulting in a draw.

Chess Strategy Tips

Opening Principles

  • Control the center of the board with pawns and pieces
  • Develop your knights and bishops early
  • Castle early to protect your king
  • Connect your rooks by clearing the back rank
  • Avoid moving the same piece multiple times in the opening

Middle Game Tactics

  • Look for forks, pins, and skewers
  • Create and exploit weaknesses in your opponent's position
  • Maintain piece coordination and activity
  • Balance attack and defense
  • Plan several moves ahead when possible

Endgame Essentials

  • Activate your king in the endgame
  • Push passed pawns toward promotion
  • Know basic checkmate patterns (K+Q vs K, K+R vs K)
  • Understand the opposition concept with kings
  • Create zugzwang positions when possible

Chess History

Chess originated in northern India during the Gupta Empire around the 6th century AD, where it was known as "chaturanga." From India, the game spread to Persia, where it evolved and became known as "shatranj."

After the Islamic conquest of Persia, chess spread to the Muslim world and subsequently to Southern Europe. By the 15th century, the game had largely evolved into its current form, with the major adjustments being the addition of the queen's expanded movement capabilities.

The first modern chess tournament was held in London in 1851, won by Adolf Anderssen. The first official World Chess Championship match was held in 1886, where Wilhelm Steinitz defeated Johannes Zukertort to become the first undisputed World Chess Champion.

Chess reached a new milestone in 1997 when IBM's Deep Blue computer defeated World Champion Garry Kasparov. Today, chess continues to evolve both as a competitive sport and a recreational activity, with millions of players worldwide and advanced AI opponents available online.
